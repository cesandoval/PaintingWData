Introducing Painting with Data

A new software platform that allows you to learn-by-doing and create your own spatial models.

Overview

Painting with Data was born out of a long brewing frustration at seeing students and professionals alike struggle to learn and make use of spatial data.

Current geographic information system (GIS) software are often not intuitive and present steep learning curves, requiring programming knowledge as well as acceptance of many embedded assumptions.

Nevertheless, increasingly, geo-spatial data has become a standard part of decision-making in both the public and private sectors.

From architecture to public health, data scientists and researchers are expected to work with spatial data and create maps that often stand on many unknowns. Only few and far between become “experts” and have ready access to such tools and capabilities.

Thus, we at Painting with Data sought to learn more about the roots and history of GIS and mapping and further understand how we as humans learn and create.

It was then that we decided that the geospatial paradigm had to be challenged.

First, we decided that Painting with Data would allow users to directly manipulate the map itself, instead of operating as a database paired with a map or a graphical representation as traditional mapping software.

Second, Painting with Data utilizes voxels, which are a two-dimensional representation of a three-dimensional overlay, to compute different variables into a single map, instead of traditionally-used vectors.

Third, Painting with Data provides a visual coding interface through drag-and-drop building blocks which allows for ‘real-time’ geospatial computing, instead of the use of textual programming.

Through an easy-to-use online interface, users can create spatial models that allow them to iteratively think about the correlations among datasets and build spatial models on the fly.

At Painting with Data, we work to bridge the gap between geospatial mapping and human learning and bring forth a tool that can be practical and useful for anyone and everyone interested in creating their own spatial models.

We hope you’re as excited as we are to play and discover new ways of seeing and engaging with spatial data.
